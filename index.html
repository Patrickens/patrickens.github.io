About Me Publications CV Blog

I am a computational biologist and machine learning researcher interested in making biological systems more predictable and useful. I work at the interface of mechanistic modeling and modern ML, with a focus on metabolic and process modeling, simulation-based inference, and decision-making under uncertainty.

I completed my PhD in computational biology at ETH Zurich, where I worked on simulation-based metabolic flux inference. My research focused on Bayesian inference of biochemical reaction rates and on developing generative modeling methods for constrained biological systems, giving me a strong foundation in probabilistic modeling and scientific software development.

Most recently, as an independent contractor at Differential Bio, I developed differentiable dynamic metabolic simulators and inference pipelines for microbial bioprocesses — described in detail in this blog post. In parallel, I built a pipeline for automatic genome-scale metabolic model reconstruction and curation, which led to the company's first commercial software license and is now used to recommend growth media and to screen microbial libraries for commercialization.

I am open to contractor work and always looking for opportunities to collaborate — feel free to reach out if you are working on something at the intersection of mechanistic modeling, ML, and biological systems.

Outside of work, I spend a lot of time in the mountains - climbing and ski touring whenever possible. I also enjoy reading and playing guitar, usually alternating between trying to learn something new and just enjoying the process.

    My contract at Differential Bio focused on building reliable modeling infrastructure that teams could use in production, not just in one-off research notebooks. A central part of the work was implementing differentiable dynamic metabolic simulators that could couple mechanistic constraints with modern inference methods. This meant setting up a robust modeling stack where ODE-based process models, parameter priors, and optimization loops worked together under realistic data quality and throughput constraints. I spent significant effort on numerical stability and reproducibility so that model updates remained trustworthy as new fermentation runs were added. In parallel, I designed and implemented an automated genome-scale model reconstruction and curation workflow to reduce manual turnaround time for new organisms. That pipeline translated fragmented inputs into usable models with quality checks and curation hooks, making it practical for repeated use across projects. The outcome was not only faster internal development cycles but also a direct commercial impact: the resulting software capabilities contributed to the company's first commercial license. The same framework is now applied to media recommendation and candidate screening workflows, where practical decision support and maintainability matter as much as model sophistication.

    During my PhD at ETH Zurich, I focused on turning metabolic flux inference into a scalable probabilistic workflow that could handle realistic biological complexity. The research combined Bayesian modeling, constrained simulation, and robust software implementation. A recurring challenge was that inference quality depended as much on numerical behavior and model structure as on the statistical method itself. I worked on this end-to-end: defining priors that respected biochemical constraints, building inference strategies for noisy experimental data, and designing methods that remained stable when scaling from toy examples to real datasets. On the methodological side, I developed generative modeling approaches for constrained biological systems, including work on flows over convex polytopes motivated by flux geometry. On the application side, I built mass-spectrometry data pipelines and Bayesian inference workflows for biochemical reaction rates. A key engineering output was the sbmfi package, implemented in PyTorch, to make these methods usable and extensible for repeated experiments. The broader objective was practical: reduce dependence on ad hoc analysis and make uncertainty-aware metabolic inference reproducible enough to support biological and process decisions. That blend of theory, implementation, and applied validation strongly shaped how I now approach modeling problems in industry.
